Interactive Roadmap for Intellectual Freedom Alternative Spring Break Project
Interactive educational tool that helps users learn about intellectual freedom



Project Description

I spent my spring break as a UX intern at the Office for Intellectual Freedom (OIF) at the American Library Association, Chicago as a part of the Alternative Spring Break 2017 program by University of Michigan. The office wanted to create a roadmap for individuals who sought to learn about Intellectual Freedom and wanted to help them with their research. To solve this, I designed an interactive educational tool backed by short-spanned research conducted with the help of user interviews, a survey and affinity mapping.

Project Details

  • When: Feb to Mar 2017
  • Where: American Library Association, Chicago
  • Role: UX Researcher, Interaction Designer
  • Project Type: Individual
  • Time frame: 1 week

Problem

The problem at hand was to find a way to help users learn about intellectual freedom in a hassle free manner. To address, the Office for Intellectual Freedom wanted to create an interactive roadmap. The nature of the project was abstract and a lot had to be done within a span of a week. During my research, I learnt about the shortcomings of the website and expectations from users who are eager to learn about intellectual freedom. Users wanted to learn a lot more about intellectual freedom but did not know where to start. They wanted a step-by-step learning tool that helped them explore a topic in a much more consistent manner. My research showed that there was an information overload on the current website and how users craved for a more intuitive and humanistic web experience.

Target Audience

Librarians who want to learn about Intellectual Freedom (IF) to help them tackle book challenge attempts and other IF issues

Users who are curious to learn about IF

Target Solution

Since the project was abstract, I had to break down tasks into sizeable chunks in order to simplify the problem. I started the project by conducting user interviews that revealed some of the primary concerns about the current website and what people wished to see. Soon after that, I designed and circulated a survey to understand people’s level of awareness about Intellectual Freedom, services offered by OIF and expectations from their website. I conducted some independent research by interpreting social media data and reading documentation with content on the proposed interactive intellectual roadmap. I analyzed data from both qualitative and quantitative research methods to gain insights and come up with recommendations. Finally, I came up with an initial Intellectual Freedom Roadmap design that the office was looking for. In addition to these, I was able to point out certain usability concerns with respect to responsive design, certain web elements, learning goals and section demarcations. Apart from the design and recommendations, I gave the team a broader set of ideas that they could work on, in the future.

Findings

  • People are lost trying to find information
  • People want to learn a lot more about Intellectual Freedom and don’t know where to start
  • Lack of awareness - Banned Books Initiative is the most popular one
  • People are concerned about privacy
  • People want a better way to access information on the site
  • People like a step-by-step guide to information
  • People use mobile devices a lot
  • People prefer text and images over links and video
  • People want a human touch to the site

Recommendations

  • Break down information into chunks for easy read
  • Organize the content into sections - Learn, Collaborate, Engage/Lead/Inspire, Connect, Support
  • Create an F.A.Q. page
  • Engage users on social media via questions like “What does Intellectual Freedom mean to you?”, “What do you like the most”, “What do you want to learn about” and display the live data feed
  • Add a report button
  • Include case interpretation stories
  • Make the site responsive so that it is accessible on all devices and platforms

UX Research

In order to understand the problem, I conducted independent research where I read through the documentation and project proposal, scoured social media to understand user interactions and behavior, conducted user interviews and circulated a survey. I analysed all the information before I could design the roadmap.


Design




Future Scope

  • Progress Visualization - Timeline view of where they should go next indicating progress
  • Test your knowledge about IF - Quiz to check their knowledge about Intellectual Freedom and level-based system (beginner, intermediate, expert)
  • Geo-location based activities - Show what libraries nearby are doing to encourage people to collaborate and learn in a community setting
  • Infographics - Graphical representation of “Top 10 Books” over the years and checklists
  • Stories and Cases - Interpretations of challenge attempts, successful and unsuccessful challenges in the past

Future Scope

I applied concepts that I had learnt in class to address a real problem that the Office for Intellectual Freedom wanted to solve. Employees who walked past my desk, stopped to ask questions as they were intrigued by the colorful post-its that I had used to create an affinity map and my hand-drawn sketches. They were excited to learn more and I was more than happy to teach them some of the design methods that I learn at the university. As the sole project owner, I had to juggle user experience research and design aspects within a week and I was pleasantly surprised by the final project outcome. This experience is something that I will remember for life!
