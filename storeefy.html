STOReeFY Interaction Design Project
Interactive Storytelling through gamification of cultural folklore

STOReeFY introduces an interactive way of listening to age-old stories.

  • Wide collection of interactive stories to choose from
  • Curated story albums created from in-game tasks within the interactive story
  • Popular stories based on what your peers are engaging with

Details

  • When: Sept 2016 to Dec 2016
  • Where: University of Michigan, Ann Arbor
  • Role: Interaction Designer
  • Project Type: Individual
  • Time frame: 12 weeks

Problem

With the advent of technology, there is a widening generation gap that is observed around the world. Right from early years, children are exposed to popular culture and media that is resulting in neglect towards culture awareness. Cultural folklore and tales are limited to places of cultural interest or school events. Age-old storytelling that was a means of mutual bonding is no longer seen. Due to relatively few topics in common, conversations are non-existent between tech-challenged grandparents and folklore-challenged grandkids.

Target Audience

Young kids and older adults

Essentially anyone eager to have fun, learn about folklore and tales

Target Solution

In order to ensure a shared mutual experience between older folk and kids, instill cultural awareness in kids and familiarize older generation with technology, Storeefy introduces interactive storytelling through gamification of cultural folklore.

Try this interactive prototype below

Features

1. Rich collection of stories to choose from
2. In-game activities and rewards to keep you engaged
3. Story Moments to see how you aced the game
4. Personal story albums that you can share with your friends and family
5. Get custom results using our search and filters feature
6. Popular Stories section to see what your peers are engaging with
7. Recommendations based on your interests
8. History section to view how many stories you have learnt over time

Process

Problem scoping

This stage involved independent research about the problem, reading papers and articles about the problem, competitor analysis and brainstorming. I interviewed older adults with children and grandchildren, either living with them or away from them, a couple with a young son, kids who were under the age of 5. All the interviews were conducted after taking a consent from the kids' parents. My key takeaway was how any given problem impacts multiple stakeholders and as a designer, I should learn to address all their concerns. Competitor analysis showed me how there have been some efforts in the past in this direction.





Exploring the solution space

This stage involved brainstorming individually and with peers to understand the problem better. I found peer feedback extremely useful as it gave me different perspectives to the problem that I was addressing. It was during these sessions that I realised that this problem is evident around the world and something has to be done about it. In order to make my divergent solutions more realistic, I sketched it all out. I started with a single solution and then progressed to 3 solutions and created personas based on my preliminary research and interviews. I learnt how looking at a problem using personas changed the way I addressed the problem.





Finding a good solution

This was an iterative process and involved storyboarding, brainstorming and design critique sessions, persona creation, user research and testing and refining solutions based on user feedback. At the end of this stage, I had a better idea of what my users thought the problem was and something that echoed out loud was "You are not your own user always." "Listen to the user." "There is no right answer."







This was followed by brainstorming sessions which involved peer design critique and feedback. Throughout the design process, there was peer discussion to understand the loophooles in each other's design and this design critique was extremely useful to refine the solution. Additionally, the ethnically diverse peer feedback team helped me gain deep cultural insights from people around the world.






Refining the solution

Based on my design rationale and insights from my user interviews, I narrowed down to prototyping the 2nd solution. My initial assumption was that solution 1 or solution 3 would be the best solutions, however, I was wrong. I learnt that through my interviews and design critiques. Soon after, I was involved in building prototypes of varying degrees. I started off with pen and paper for my low-fidelity prototype and then moved on to tools like Sketch and Invision to build my high-fidelity prototypes. The image here shows the entire paper prototype that was built for this project.



High-fidelity prototypes

Paper prototyping is fun! I learnt how paper prototyping is an inexpensive way to test a design. After duly noting down mistakes from the paper prototyping phase, I decided to create my high-fidelity prototype. This was built using tools like Sketch and Invision.
