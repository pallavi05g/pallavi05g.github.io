Storeefy

Bridging inter-generational gap and igniting conversations through interactive storytelling


Designed an interactive storytelling app that involves gamified approach to learning by following a user centered approach of brainstorming, interviewing, persona creation and rapid prototyping

What is the problem at hand?

With the advent of technology, there is a widening generation gap that is observed around the world. Exposure to popular media since early formative years is changing the cultural landscape. In certain cultures, age-old storytelling that was a means of mutual bonding is no longer seen and is restricted to places of cultural interest. Due to relatively few topics in common, conversations have become non-existent between tech-challenged grandparents and folklore-challenged grandkids.

How does Storeefy help?

In order to create shared experiences between older folk and kids, instill cultural awareness in kids and familiarize older generation with technology, Storeefy introduces an interactive way of listening to age-old stories. It leverages gamification of cultural folklore to bring families closer.

How does it do so?

1. Nurtures bonding by fostering shared experiences
2. Blends authentic storytelling with gamified elements
3. Introduces a friendly character that kids can connect with
4. Incorporates tasks and rewards
5. Creates story albums based on engagement
6. Simplifies content for easy understanding

My Design Process

Problem scoping

Kids preferred to interact with technology. Older adults such as grandparents preferred hardcover books and weren’t very familiar with technology. Older adults were keen to connect with their grandkids, nephews or nieces in a manner that doesn’t seem too forced. This user group also expressed concerns about relatively few topics in common, increasing family disconnect and fragmentation apart from the loss of family bonding time.

User research showed that these groups had opposite needs and there was very little in common. However, one thing that stood out was how both these groups enjoyed games and had short attention spans.

Solution Exploration

I started by exploring solutions spanning media types such as smart toys, role-playing games and even community learning sessions. This exercise gave me some unique insights to the problem at hand.



1. Toy: Not feasible for kids of all ages and easily breakable; kids (even those aged 3) tend to smash their toys and are aware that anything tech needs to be handled carefully.
2. Upload pictures based on prompts: Too much ground work and is not travel friendly.
3. Role-playing: Hassle as it requires a dedicated space and prior preparation.
4. Graphic Novels: Not engaging enough as it only gives content and users are mute consumers of content.
5. Podcasts and Radio Talkshows: No visual element, can get uninteresting for this age group together, short attention spans.
6. Movie screenings: Restricted to school trips/culture tours.
7. Community Storytelling sessions: Restricted to summer camps, classes and libraries, emphasis on social interaction with peers and not in-sync with our goal.
8. Library: Quiet setting and no interaction with others.

In conclusion, none of these initial solutions worked as is. Research interviews with user(s) showed that anything that would be on a mobile device such as smartphones or tablets would be the most suitable. The final solution was a mobile application that borrowed elements from a few of these solutions above and added some elements from user research data.

Design Rationale

Blends authentic storytelling with gamified elements

Both kids and older adults really liked the fact that they could get really involved with the app and take breaks when necessary to talk about the story at a deeper level.

Introducing a friendly character that kids can connect with

Parents of these kids wanted to preserve these shared experiences in some format that they could look back to.


Incorporating tasks and rewards

Kids were given candy or appreciated when they performed a task in real-life. Taking that concept into the app worked. Another reason for splitting a story into multiple levels was because of the short attention spans. By breaking down the story into levels similar to chapters in books, users were able to consume information at the rate that they were used to.

Creates story albums based on engagement

Kids really liked this concept and showed higher engagement levels with the app.
