wayForward Usability and Heuristic Evaluation
Needs Assessment and Usability Evaluation Client Project

Description

As external User Experience Researchers for a period of 12 weeks, we investigated the stress-anxiety management tool called wayForward through a research process that comprised of interaction maps, interviews, comparative analysis, surveys, heuristic evaluation and usability tests.

Details

  • When: Jan 2017 to April 2017
  • Where: PsyInnovations, New York and University of Michigan, Ann Arbor
  • Role: User Experience Researcher
  • Project Type: Group
  • Time frame: 12 weeks

Problem

During the beginning of the semester, we were assigned an external client for the semester and a project. Our client, PsyInnovations, wanted us to evaluate their product, wayForward, a stress-anxiety management application based on Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). It is available as web, Android and iOS versions and follows a subscription-based model. wayForward's sessions were categorized as - Interviews, Public Speaking, Social Gathering and Dating. Apart from these, the app also offers a set of tools to help track behaviors, worries, thoughts, body sensation and offers breathing exercises.

From our stakeholder interview, we learned PsyInnovations hopes to enhance the user experience and interface of WayForward. The program generally receives positive feedback from users who complete the required initial sessions and hopes to convey the value proposition to the users in an early stage. The users want to see the result of improvement in a short period of time, so our client wanted us to investigate how to visualize their progress and ensure long-term use of the application. As UX Researchers, our primary goal was to identify ways to help improve the user engagement of the wayForward application by evaluating the usability of the features that it offers.

Target Solution

Over a period of 12 weeks, our user experience research project involved interaction maps, user interviews, comparative analysis, surveys, heuristic evaluations and usability studies. The project involved periodic check-ins with our client, elaborate weekly project reports and presentations.

Process

Process Overview

1. Conducted a stakeholder interview to understand the problem that the company is trying to solve
2. Designed personas to identify problems, behaviors and goals of target users based on user interviews
3. Created an interaction maps to capture every page in the application, understand task flows, user interactions with the application be it login or signup, goal setting, therapy sessions and tool usage
4. Conducted qualitative research in-depth interviews with target users that followed the Think-Aloud Protocol
5. Collected data points from our quantitative research survey methodology
6. Performed an in-depth competitive analysis
7. Each of us performed heuristic evaluation of the app, wayForward, as per Nielson's Heuristic Principles
8. Conducted 5 usability studies, each comprising a pre-test questionnaire, three scenarios and a post-test survey (System Usability Scale survey).

Findings

1. Discoverability: The application has a linear workflow that prevents users from accessing content when needed
2. Payment: People do not currently perceive the information in stress management applications worth paying for
3. Content Strategy: Inconsistent word usage and scientific vocabulary unexplained
4. Clarity: Lack of detailed instructions within the tool thereby confusing users
5. Simplicity: People want to access information as images or short video clips
6. Learnability: People want to see progress and track it across goals
7. Perception: Tools and exercises provide novel material that users are interested in. However, the sessions were viewed as dense because of the large amount of text.
8. Credibility: People want to know why this is better than the rest of the free online information
9. Relevancy: People have different definitions of stress and the app must cater to all the target users
10. User Control and Freedom: Lack of customization flexibility within the application, like the daily length and difficulty of exercises


Recommendations

1. Discoverability: Allow users to have more control; utilize home menu.
2. Payment: Charge advanced features
3. Content Strategy: Differentiate tools by changing the names and add more effective description
4. Clarity: Less text and more media like images and videos; incorporate tooltips to guide a first-time user
5. Simplicity: Use patterns and recognition in tools and sessions to help users to remember what or how they did an activity previously
6. Learnability: Utilize simple visual elements (videos, images) in each session to facilitate the learning phase and to minimize the cognitive load; make the design intuitive
7. Credibility: Incorporate an about us or reference page; introduce the research team and provide expert testimony within the application
8. Relevancy: Implement usersâ€™ two stress management approaches (flight vs. fight) in the app.
9. User Control and Freedom: Make the session and information related to CBT be accessible outside of sessions. 10. Relevancy: Include tools that can be used as temporary distractions from stress

Limitations

1. Did not evaluate desktop platform
2. Comparison with the Coach app would have yielded different results
3. Did not have access to current user base due to confidentiality constraints
4. Focused on stress

My Takeaways

Working on this semester long project as a UX Researcher has helped me understand how research is an ongoing process and a product that is well designed has gone through rigorous research, design and testing iterations. I also learnt how observation is the key to being a good UX Researcher.
